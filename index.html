Research & Projects

TD with Linear Function Approximation in State Aggregated Setting ▼

The Temporal Difference (TD) algorithm is at the heart of some of the most successful examples of reinforcement learning (RL). However, it has been shown that it may exhibit divergent behavior. I aimed to demystify the relationship between convergence and state representations in a simplified setting (linear function approximation and state aggregation). I wrote it as a technical report in a post.

Energy Transfer Strategies in Magnetic Resonance–Based Intrabody Networks (WoWMoM, 2025) ▼

We study a general resource allocation problem in Chronic Disease Management Networks (CDMNs), where a single on-body node must distribute limited wireless power, via Magnetic Resonance Communication (MRC), across multiple implanted hubs and sensors over long time horizons. Using a detailed, calibrated energy-centric simulation of CDMNs, we compare three energy allocation policies: a centralized scheme that uses global state to jointly select which nodes harvest in each round, a fully decentralized scheme in which nodes make probabilistic harvesting decisions based only on their local energy state and simple trajectory estimates, and a hybrid policy that combines aspects of both. Our results show that centralized allocation can maintain higher energy levels at critical hub nodes under nominal conditions, but is sensitive to modeling and parameter errors and can be brittle under adverse scenarios such as supply fluctuations or demand spikes. Decentralized control greatly reduces coordination overhead and improves robustness to such adverse events, at the cost of some efficiency, while the hybrid policy recovers much of the centralized performance without sacrificing robustness. Overall, the work illustrates how different classes of resource allocation policies trade off efficiency, robustness, and overhead in small, safety-critical intrabody networks. The results are published in World of Wireless, Mobile and Multimedia Networks (WoWMoM) 2025.

I am currently working on an extension of this work that formulates the subset-selection problem as a contextual bandit. Instead of repeatedly solving a model-based optimization with a fixed path-loss approximation, the on-body node learns a Linear Thompson Sampling policy over receiver subsets, using per-node energy state and predicted gain as context and observed post-transfer energies as feedback. This bandit-style strategy reduces reliance on imperfect models, adapts to non-stationary channel conditions, and empirically outperforms the centralized, decentralized, and hybrid baselines in terms of maintaining balanced energy across the network while keeping computation tractable.

Energy–Accuracy Tradeoff in Intra‑body Networks (CHASE, 2025) ▼

Chronic Disease Management Networks (CDMNs) need to minimize communication energy over Magnetic Resonance Communication (MRC) links while preserving physiological signal fidelity for hub-side monitoring and actuation. In our paper, we introduce a closed-loop Kalman-filter controller where the hub predicts sensor readings and dynamically tunes per-sensor transmission thresholds based on reconstruction error, sending samples (or batches) only when predictions would deviate too far. Simulations on multi-sensor links show 30–67% reductions in sensor–hub communication with 88–94% reconstruction accuracy (CHASE 2025).

I am extending this into a general selective communication framework with three components: a sensor classifier that gates transmissions, a hub predictor that reconstructs missing samples from mixed true/imputed history, and a manager that updates the gating threshold. The extension replaces ad‑hoc rules with two principled schemes—instantaneous-error using innovation-energy statistics and log-MSE feedback, and cumulative-error based on CUSUM drift detection—for stable, tunable control along the energy–accuracy tradeoff.

SINDy+RL for Pendulum and Nonstationary Oscillator Control ▼

This project investigates a SINDy-RL approach where Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy) is used to learn compact surrogate models that drive model-based reinforcement learning controllers. Using a classic swing-up pendulum and a second-order oscillator with time-varying stiffness and damping, I train SINDy from limited interaction data and embed it in a receding-horizon planner that optimizes action sequences over the learned dynamics. On the pendulum, I compare online and offline SINDy-RL to show how continuous model adaptation enables successful swing-up under noisy observations, whereas a fixed model learned from a small dataset can converge to the wrong equilibrium. On the nonstationary oscillator, I compare online SINDy-RL against a neural-network dynamics model and a model-free DQN agent: SINDy-RL matches the NN controller's low tracking error (MSE ≈ 0.18 vs. 0.17) while maintaining interpretable stiffness and damping coefficients, and outperforms DQN by roughly two orders of magnitude in tracking error (MSE ≈ 11.15), highlighting its robustness to drifting dynamics and its data efficiency.

Augmenting Optimizers with NARS: A Learning Rate Scheduler ▼

I designed the NARS Learning Rate Scheduler, which integrates the Non-Axiomatic Reasoning System (NARS) with gradient-based optimization to adapt learning rates through explicit logical reasoning rather than fixed heuristics. The scheduler treats learning rate selection as a sequential decision problem: it encodes training signals (loss trajectory, gradient norms, validation performance) as NARS beliefs with frequency–confidence truth values, then uses syllogistic inference to choose phase-specific update strategies for exploration, exploitation, and fine-tuning. Experiments on the Rosenbrock function and deep networks trained on Fashion-MNIST and CIFAR-10 show consistent improvements over standard schedulers such as fixed-rate and OneCycle (e.g., roughly 75% lower final loss on Rosenbrock, and accuracy gains of about 3.2% on Fashion-MNIST and 1.1% on CIFAR-10), while providing interpretable explanations for each learning-rate adjustment. This project illustrates how symbolic reasoning can serve as a meta-optimizer for deep learning, bridging logical AI with numerical optimization.

Macroeconomy Dynamics Discovery using SINDyC (Computational Economics, 2025) ▼

We develop an explainable nonlinear dynamical model of key macroeconomic variables using Transfer Entropy (TE) for variable selection and Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics with control (SINDyC). Using quarterly data from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) from 1960 to 2022, the model captures the joint dynamics of unemployment, inflation, and GDP per capita, with the interest rate acting as a policy-manipulated input. SINDyC learns a sparse system of differential equations that both fits out-of-sample data and remains interpretable, revealing which interactions (e.g., inflation–GDP couplings) drive the evolution of each variable. Phase-plane analysis of the learned system identifies stability regions for different combinations of interest rate and GDP per capita, offering a qualitative map of regimes where unemployment and inflation remain stable versus where they tend to drift. This work is published in Computational Economics.

Autotuning in MRC circuits (DCOSS, 2024) ▼

The resonance between a transmitter and receiver is critical in efficient power transfer or data communication. We created an adaptive controller that basically solves a quasiconvex optimization problem with a zeroth-order oracle, simply and efficiently. We consider capacitance as the control signal and used zeroth-order gradient estimation to drive the system into resonance. The results were published in DCOSS 2024.

As an extension, we are considering a network-wide retuning of transmitter and receivers, where detuning results in a cascading failure of the current method due to the natural redistribution of energy. By changing the trigger from the absolute change to ReLU, we are making sure the redistribution would not cause an undesirable activation of the retuners.

Bayesian-optimized Neural Net for Adsorption modeling (JTICE, 2023) ▼

I used a relatively simple NN (2 layered) with Huber loss objective function to model adsorption process of tetracycline removal by multi-walled carbon nanotube functionalized with aspartic acid and poly-pyrrole. Then we implemented Bayesian Optimization with RMSE cost function as the hyperparameter tuning scheme. For decision variables, I chose First Layer Neuron Count, Second Layer Neuron Count, Huber Constant, Learning Rate, First Layer Activation function, First Layer Activation function, Batch size, Dropout, The exponential decay rate for the 1st moment estimates and exponential decay rate for the exponentially weighted infinity norm (last two were hyperparameters of the optimizer NADAM). The results were published in Journal of the Taiwan Institute of Chemical Engineers.

Multi-Library SINDy: A Computationally Efficient Framework for SINDy (In Progress) ▼

We showed that although SINDy is very efficient in finding the dynamics, however there is no need for some of the constraints, such as a fixed library function for all the states. We showed using Multi-Library SINDy is more efficient in terms of computations and it can provide additional freedom in choosing and guiding the identification process.
