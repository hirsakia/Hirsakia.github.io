TD with Linear Function Approximation in State Aggregated Setting ▼
  • Temporal Difference (TD) learning is central to many successful reinforcement learning methods but can diverge under function approximation.
  • I study how TD convergence depends on state representation in a simplified setting with linear function approximation and state aggregation.
  • The goal is to clarify when particular aggregations preserve or break TD’s convergence guarantees in practice.
  • I wrote up the analysis and examples in a technical report, available as a blog post.
Energy Transfer Strategies in Intrabody Networks (WoWMoM, 2025) ▼
  • Problem: A single on-body node must distribute limited wireless power via Magnetic Resonance Communication (MRC) to multiple implanted hubs and sensors over long horizons.
  • Approach: We build a detailed, calibrated energy-centric simulation of Chronic Disease Management Networks (CDMNs) and compare three allocation strategies.
  • Centralized: Uses global state to select harvesting nodes each round, achieving high energy levels at critical hubs under nominal conditions but showing brittleness under model errors, supply fluctuations, and demand spikes.
  • Decentralized: Lets nodes make probabilistic harvesting decisions from local energy state and simple trajectory estimates, improving robustness and lowering coordination overhead at some efficiency cost.
  • Hybrid: Combines centralized and decentralized elements, recovering much of the centralized performance without sacrificing robustness.
  • Takeaway: The study highlights how centralized, decentralized, and hybrid policies trade off efficiency, robustness, and control overhead in small, safety-critical intrabody networks.
  • Publication: Results appear in World of Wireless, Mobile and Multimedia Networks (WoWMoM) 2025.
  • Ongoing work: I am extending this by formulating the subset-selection problem as a contextual bandit, where the on-body node learns a Linear Thompson Sampling policy over receiver subsets.
  • Bandit idea: The policy uses per-node energy state and predicted gain as context and observed post-transfer energies as feedback, reducing reliance on imperfect path-loss models and adapting to non-stationary channels.
  • Empirical result: This bandit-style strategy maintains more balanced energy across the network and outperforms the centralized, decentralized, and hybrid baselines while keeping computation tractable.
Energy–Accuracy Tradeoff in Intra‑body Networks (CHASE, 2025) ▼
  • Problem: Chronic Disease Management Networks (CDMNs) must minimize communication energy over MRC links while preserving physiological signal fidelity for monitoring and actuation at the hub.
  • Published work: We design a closed-loop Kalman-filter controller where the hub predicts sensor readings and adjusts per-sensor transmission thresholds based on reconstruction error.
  • Mechanism: Sensors send samples (or batches) only when the hub’s prediction error exceeds a threshold, reducing transmissions while controlling reconstruction quality.
  • Result: Multi-sensor simulations show 30–67% reductions in sensor–hub communication with 88–94% reconstruction accuracy (CHASE 2025).
  • Extension: I am generalizing this into a selective communication framework with three components—a sensor-side classifier that gates transmissions, a hub-side predictor that reconstructs missing samples, and a manager that updates thresholds.
  • Control schemes: The manager uses two principled strategies, an instantaneous-error scheme based on innovation-energy statistics and log-MSE feedback, and a cumulative-error scheme based on CUSUM-style drift detection.
  • Goal: Provide stable, tunable control along the energy–accuracy tradeoff while decoupling prediction from decision logic.
SINDy+RL for Pendulum and Nonstationary Oscillator Control ▼
  • Goal: Combine Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy) with model-based reinforcement learning to control nonlinear, possibly nonstationary systems.
  • Systems: A classic swing-up pendulum and a second-order oscillator with time-varying stiffness and damping.
  • Method: Learn compact SINDy surrogate models from limited interaction data and embed them in a receding-horizon planner that optimizes action sequences.
  • Pendulum findings: Compare offline (fixed) and online (adaptive) SINDy-RL, showing that continuous model adaptation enables robust swing-up under noisy observations, whereas a small fixed model can converge to the wrong equilibrium.
  • Nonstationary oscillator: Benchmark online SINDy-RL against a neural-network dynamics model and a model-free DQN agent.
  • Performance: SINDy-RL matches the neural controller’s low tracking error (MSE ≈ 0.18 vs. 0.17) while keeping interpretable stiffness and damping coefficients.
  • Robustness: SINDy-RL outperforms DQN by roughly two orders of magnitude in tracking error (MSE ≈ 11.15), illustrating robustness to drifting dynamics and data efficiency.

Augmenting Optimizers with NARS: A Learning Rate Scheduler ▼
  • Idea: Use the Non-Axiomatic Reasoning System (NARS) as a learning-rate scheduler that reasons explicitly about training signals instead of relying on fixed heuristics.
  • Viewpoint: Treat learning-rate selection as a sequential decision problem over optimization phases (exploration, exploitation, fine-tuning).
  • Representation: Encode loss trajectories, gradient norms, and validation performance as NARS beliefs with frequency–confidence truth values.
  • Inference: Use syllogistic reasoning in NARS to select phase-specific learning-rate update strategies at each step.
  • Experiments: Evaluate on the Rosenbrock function and deep networks trained on Fashion-MNIST and CIFAR-10.
  • Results: Achieve roughly 75% lower final loss on Rosenbrock and accuracy gains of about 3.2% on Fashion-MNIST and 1.1% on CIFAR-10 over standard schedulers such as fixed-rate and OneCycle.
  • Benefit: The scheduler provides interpretable explanations for each learning-rate adjustment, illustrating how symbolic reasoning can act as a meta-optimizer for deep learning.
Macroeconomy Dynamics Discovery using SINDyC (Computational Economics, 2025) ▼
  • Objective: Build an explainable nonlinear dynamical model of key macroeconomic variables.
  • Data: Quarterly FRED data from 1960–2022 for unemployment, inflation, GDP per capita, and interest rate.
  • Variable selection: Use Transfer Entropy (TE) to select informative variables and couplings.
  • Modeling: Apply Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics with control (SINDyC) to learn a sparse system of differential equations with the interest rate as a policy input.
  • Interpretability: Reveal which interactions (e.g., inflation–GDP couplings) drive the evolution of each variable while maintaining good out-of-sample fit.
  • Analysis: Use phase-plane analysis to identify stability regions across combinations of interest rate and GDP per capita.
  • Insight: Provide a qualitative map of regimes where unemployment and inflation remain stable versus where they drift.
  • Publication: Work appears in Computational Economics.
Autotuning in MRC circuits (DCOSS, 2024) ▼
  • Problem: Efficient power transfer and data communication over MRC links require maintaining resonance between transmitter and receiver circuits.
  • Approach: Design an adaptive controller that solves a quasiconvex optimization problem using a zeroth-order oracle.
  • Control signal: Treat capacitance as the control variable and use zeroth-order gradient estimation to drive the system toward resonance.
  • Result: Demonstrate simple, efficient autotuning behavior in MRC circuits, published in DCOSS 2024.
  • Extension: Study network-wide retuning of multiple transmitters and receivers, where detuning can cause cascading failures due to energy redistribution.
  • Fix: Change the retuning trigger from absolute change to a ReLU-based criterion to prevent benign redistribution from causing undesirable activations.
Bayesian-optimized Neural Net for Adsorption modeling (JTICE, 2023) ▼
  • Problem: Model adsorption process for tetracycline removal using multi-walled carbon nanotubes functionalized with aspartic acid and poly-pyrrole.
  • Approach: Train a two-layer neural network with Huber loss as the objective function.
  • Hyperparameter optimization: Use Bayesian Optimization with RMSE as the cost function.
  • Decision variables: First/second layer neuron counts, Huber constant, learning rate, layer activation functions, batch size, dropout rate, and NADAM optimizer parameters (β₁ and β₂ decay rates).
  • Publication: Results appear in Journal of the Taiwan Institute of Chemical Engineers (JTICE) 2023.
Multi-Library SINDy: Computationally Efficient Variant of SINDy (In Progress) ▼
  • Observation: Standard SINDy often uses a single fixed library of candidate functions for all state variables, which can be unnecessarily restrictive.
  • Idea: Introduce Multi-Library SINDy, allowing different state variables to use different, tailored function libraries.
  • Benefit: Reduce computational cost by avoiding large, monolithic libraries while improving flexibility in guiding the identification process.
  • Goal: Maintain SINDy’s efficiency in discovering governing equations while giving practitioners more control over model structure and inductive biases.
